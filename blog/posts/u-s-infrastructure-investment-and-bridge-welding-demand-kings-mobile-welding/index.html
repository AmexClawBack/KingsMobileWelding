America’s Bridge Network Is Aging

The United States has more than 623,000 bridges, and while many are still safe and serviceable, a large portion of the bridge network is aging, worn, or in need of repair. ASCE’s 2025 Infrastructure Report Card shows that 6.8% of U.S. bridges are in poor condition, while nearly half are in fair condition. That “fair” category matters because many of those structures can decline into poor condition if repairs are delayed.

ARTBA’s 2025 bridge analysis paints the same picture from another angle: more than 220,000 U.S. bridges need some level of repair, and over 41,000 are rated in poor condition. Those poor-condition bridges are crossed by motorists roughly 163 million times every day.

That does not mean every bridge is unsafe, but it does show the size of the maintenance and replacement backlog. Bridges are exposed to traffic loads, salt, moisture, vessel impact risk, freeze-thaw cycles, scour, fatigue, and years of normal wear. Eventually, steel repairs, bearing replacements, pile work, deck support repairs, girder modifications, and full replacement projects become unavoidable.

Infrastructure Funding Is Moving from Planning to Real Work

The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law created one of the largest federal infrastructure funding pushes in modern U.S. history. One of the most important pieces for the bridge industry is the Bridge Formula Program, which provides more than $27 billion to states, D.C., Puerto Rico, and tribal transportation facilities for bridge repair and replacement.

That funding is turning into real work: design packages, bridge inspections, repair scopes, emergency response work, marine access work, pile driving, steel fabrication, and field welding.

The Francis Scott Key Bridge Rebuild Shows the Scale of Modern Infrastructure Work

The Francis Scott Key Bridge collapse in Baltimore became one of the most visible infrastructure events in recent U.S. history. The rebuild is not just a local bridge replacement. It is a major transportation, shipping, and port recovery project tied to the Port of Baltimore and the broader East Coast supply chain.

Maryland officials have announced a delivery strategy that separates the Key Bridge rebuild into multiple major contracts, including marine demolition, north and south land approaches, and the main span with marine approaches. The main span package alone is expected to include a cable-stayed bridge, marine approach work, pier protection systems, and major over-water construction.

Projects like this show where the industry is heading. Large bridge and transportation projects are becoming more complex, with stronger emphasis on resilience, vessel collision protection, marine access, schedule control, inspection requirements, and coordination between engineers, general contractors, specialty subcontractors, fabricators, and certified welders.

Why Certified Welding Matters on Infrastructure Projects

Bridge and DOT work is not the same as basic repair welding. Infrastructure welding often involves strict procedure requirements, qualified welders, documentation, inspection points, safety planning, and coordination with engineers or inspectors.

On bridge and heavy civil projects, welding may be needed for:

  • Pipe pile splices
  • H-pile splices
  • Permanent casing splices
  • Girder repairs and modifications
  • Diaphragms, stiffeners, brackets, and connection repairs
  • Bearing and support steel repairs
  • Temporary works, access platforms, and trestle-related steel
  • Marine and port infrastructure repairs
  • Structural steel modifications for roadways, bridges, and industrial sites

This is where AWS-certified field welding support becomes critical. A contractor may have the equipment, manpower, and schedule, but if the welding work is not performed to the required standard, the project can face delays, rework, inspection issues, or documentation problems.

Bridge Demand Is Not Only About New Construction

A lot of infrastructure conversation focuses on billion-dollar mega projects, but the real demand is much broader. Many bridge scopes are not full replacement projects. They are repair, retrofit, reinforcement, or emergency response jobs.

Across the Mid-Atlantic and Southeast, bridge owners and contractors need help with smaller but critical scopes such as:

  • Cracked steel repairs
  • Corroded support members
  • Damaged brackets or connection plates
  • Pile repairs and splices
  • Expansion joint support steel
  • Temporary shoring and access steel
  • Load-related reinforcement
  • Marine facility repairs
  • Structural steel field modifications

These jobs may not make national headlines, but they keep infrastructure working. They also require contractors who can mobilize quickly, work safely, coordinate with site supervision, and complete welding that is ready for inspection.

The Skilled Trades Gap Will Keep Driving Demand

Infrastructure funding only matters if there are enough skilled contractors to perform the work. Bridge projects require welders, pile drivers, ironworkers, operators, laborers, fabricators, inspectors, engineers, surveyors, and project managers.

As more projects move from design into construction, the demand for skilled field labor will continue to increase. For welding contractors, that means the companies that can provide certified welders, mobile equipment, jobsite flexibility, and documentation support will be in a strong position.

For general contractors and bridge contractors, it also means reliable subcontractor relationships are going to matter more. A qualified welding subcontractor can help keep production moving when a field condition changes, a pile splice is needed, a repair detail gets issued, or a critical steel component has to be modified on-site.
