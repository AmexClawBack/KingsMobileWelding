Why Fixed Ladder Installation Needs Planning Before It Gets Bolted to the Building

A commercial fixed ladder is not just a ladder. It becomes part of the building access system. Before it gets drilled, anchored, welded, or bolted in place, the owner or contractor should understand the jobsite conditions.

Important details include:

  • Wall construction: CMU, brick, concrete, structural steel, metal building panels, framed walls, or existing parapets
  • Required ladder height: ground-to-roof, platform-to-roof, lower level to upper level, or equipment access height
  • Roof transition: parapet height, walk-through extension, roof edge condition, coping, and clearance
  • Attachment points: brackets, standoff brackets, anchors, embeds, backing plates, or support steel
  • Access equipment: boom lift, scissor lift, roof access, parking lot access, tenant coordination, or after-hours work
  • Fall protection: ladder height, ladder safety system, personal fall arrest system, cage, gate, platform, or owner requirements
  • Inspection requirements: owner, engineer, building department, OSHA, manufacturer, insurance, or property management requirements

OSHA’s fixed ladder requirements depend on the ladder height, installation date, workplace use, and fall exposure. OSHA has stated that fixed ladders over 24 feet installed after November 19, 2018 must be equipped with a personal fall arrest system or ladder safety system, and OSHA has also clarified that fixed ladders 24 feet or less above the lower level are not required to have those systems unless another fall-height condition applies. The responsible party should verify the final requirements before installation.
